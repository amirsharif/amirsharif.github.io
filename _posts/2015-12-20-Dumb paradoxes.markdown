title: "Dumb paradoxes"
date: 2015-12-21 02:44:41

Tonight I found myself near a bowl of beautiful grapes -- the kind you'd want to spend hours staring at if you were a dutch still-life painter. Like... round, and large, and vibrantly red. I felt sure they were also delicious.

But a dumb thought dawned on me. It would only make sense to eat the grape if the taste/nutrition of the grape served me greater utility than its aesthetic beauty. At the moment, I was sure of how much utility its beauty served me -- and it was not insignificant -- but I had no way to know exactly how much utility it offered in the way of taste/nutrition.

Now the dumb paradox: Were I to eat the grape, I would become aware of the utility of its taste to me, but I would no longer have the option to exchange this for its visual utility.

In the end I found myself making the decision not to eat the grapes -- it was too great a risk to give up the beauty of the grapes for the hope of some unknown taste. The sad truth is, in a week's time, this question for this particular set of grapes will be totally irrelevant -- they will have very limited utility in the way of beauty and taste when limp and old. Also sad: There are perhaps other ways in which these grapes could serve me even greater utility that I'm not aware of -- maybe in a few years' time, I will stumble upon a process by which I can turn grapes into, like, diamonds or energy with which to charge my dead cellphone and instead of using them in such a way I had decided to just eat them or stare at them. Whatever.

While we're on the subject of dumb paradoxes, another came to mind. Say there are two recently married couples (the Aryas and the Blooms). The two couples meet on a double date on their honeymoons, during which time both couples realize that there may be a mismatch. In fact, Mrs. A and Mr. B later phone each other and discuss this -- they are each concerned that there was an odd degree of chemistry between their corresponding spouses and that there is the risk their new marriages will end in divorce if their spouses spend any more time together. Mrs. A and Mr. B decide together that from that day forth, they will each have to make sure that Mr. A and Mrs. B never again come into contact, lest they truly fall in love (and dissolve their respective marriages). However, as the schemers come to this agreement, a thought occurs to them: Distance makes the heart grow fonder. What must they do -- keep their spouses away from each other or not?
